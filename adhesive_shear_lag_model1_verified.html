Adhesive Shear-Lag Teaching App, Model 1

Verified teaching version. The plastic substrate strain is imposed and kept uniform in the bonded region. The metal strip is not directly gripped and gains strain only through adhesive shear transfer.

1. Model used in this corrected version

What was corrected
In the earlier version, the plastic strain inside the bonded region was incorrectly amplified by a stiffness-ratio term. That was wrong for the teaching boundary condition you wanted.

In this corrected version:
  • the plastic strain in the bonded region is fixed at the imposed average value
  • the metal strain varies along the bonded strip
  • adhesive shear stress and slip follow from the metal strain solution
Imposed substrate strain: εsub = ΔLs / Ls
Plastic strain in bonded region: εp(x) = εsub
Metal strain: εm(x) = εsub [1 - cosh(λx) / cosh(λL/2)]
Transfer parameter: λ² = (Ga / ta) · [1 / (Em tm)]
Adhesive shear stress from metal equilibrium: τ(x) = Em tm εsub λ sinh(λx) / cosh(λL/2)

The charts always use the bonded-strip coordinate only, from -L/2 to +L/2. So if L = 80 mm, the chart x-axis should run from about -40 mm to +40 mm.

4. Automatic model checks

These checks are included to help verify that the app behaves sensibly: metal end strain should be near zero, centre adhesive shear should be near zero, and the plastic strain should equal the imposed substrate strain throughout the bonded region.

5. Key outputs

6. Chart 1: local strain in plastic and metal

The blue plastic line should now remain at the imposed strain level. The orange metal line starts near zero at the free strip ends and rises towards the centre. If the bond is long enough and the adhesive is stiff enough, the metal line approaches the blue line in the middle.

7. Chart 2: metal stress

This is the axial normal stress in the metal. It comes entirely from adhesive shear transfer because the metal itself is not gripped.

8. Chart 3: adhesive shear stress

Adhesive shear stress peaks near the strip ends and passes through zero at the centre for symmetric loading.

9. Chart 4: displacement of plastic and metal

The vertical gap between the two displacement curves is the interfacial slip. Smaller gap means smaller adhesive shear deformation.

10. Chart 5: interfacial slip

Slip is the relative displacement between plastic and metal, w = up - um. It is directly connected to adhesive shear stress through τ = Ga w / ta.

11. Chart 6: normalised metal strain

A value of 1 means the metal locally matches the imposed substrate strain. A value below 1 means transfer is incomplete at that position.

13. Expandable theory and verification notes

Assumptions
  1. Plastic substrate is directly gripped and stretched.
  2. Metal strip is shorter and is not directly gripped.
  3. Adhesive is thin and transmits shear.
  4. Plastic strain in the bonded region is approximated as the imposed average strain.
  5. Metal strain is solved from the shear-lag equation with free metal ends.
Why this is now more physically consistent
If you impose 1% strain on the plastic, the plastic curve should stay at about 1% in the bonded region. The metal does not automatically equal that everywhere, because the metal is not directly pulled by the grips. It must accumulate strain through adhesive shear transfer over a finite transfer length.
Useful limiting cases to test yourself
Try these:
  • Increase Ga or reduce ta: transfer should improve.
  • Increase L strongly: metal centre strain should approach the plastic strain.
  • Set ΔLs = 0: all fields should collapse to zero.
  • Make L very short: metal strain should remain low everywhere.
