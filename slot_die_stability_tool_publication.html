Key equations and interpretation (Newtonian, SI)

Premetered thickness

t = q/U

where q is volumetric flow per unit width (m²/s), U is web speed (m/s), and t is wet film thickness (m).

Dimensionless gap

G = H₀/t = H₀U/q

H₀ is the die-to-substrate coating gap (m). Larger G means the bead must bridge a relatively larger gap compared with the deposited film, increasing sensitivity to downstream meniscus instabilities.

Capillary number

Ca = μU/σ

μ is viscosity (Pa·s) and σ is surface tension (N/m). Larger Ca indicates viscous forcing dominates capillarity, often increasing air-entrainment risk at the upstream meniscus.

Meniscus capillary pressure (Laplace)

Δp_cap = σ(1/R₁ + 1/R₂)

This sets the pressure jump across each meniscus. Slot-die operability hinges on whether capillary pressure plus any applied pressure difference can balance viscous losses in the bead.

Slot mouth kinematics (geometry effect)

U_slot ≈ q/s, γ̇_exit ~ q/s²

s is the slot opening at the mouth (m). For Newtonian fluids the viscosity is constant, but smaller s increases hydraulic sensitivity and can aggravate cross-web non-uniformity (striping) even if the bead is nominally within the Ca–G window.

Defect mapping used here: (i) Ca ≥ Ca_air → air entrainment risk, (ii) G ≥ Gcrit(Ca) → downstream meniscus instability (ribbing/striping/breaklines). By default Gcrit uses Landau–Levich scaling (Ca^(2/3)) as a mechanistic proxy; a flexible schematic form is also available for teaching or curve fitting.

If you have a measured critical line for your specific die/substrate (from trials), you can fit the advanced parameters so the map matches your system.
